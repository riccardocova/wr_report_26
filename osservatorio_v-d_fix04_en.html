This is not a new phenomenon, but it is growing. According to the (2025), only 11% of people in Europe see friends in person every day. And according to the , 19% of young adults worldwide have no one to rely on — a figure that has been rising steadily from 14% in 2006.

We can see this in our Osservatorio too. When asked "of the 10 most important people in your life, how many have you known since childhood?", 41% answered just 1–3. Only 20% have a network of deep relationships rooted in the past.

As opportunities to meet people dwindle, one reason is that the places where it used to happen are also disappearing. The so-called "third places": pubs, cafés, community centres — all those spaces that are neither home nor work, where people used to meet without having to plan it. One voice on this topic is , professor at NYU, in his newsletter No Mercy / No Malice.

So people are looking for alternatives.

The old meeting places are disappearing, but people have not stopped looking for spaces where they can feel part of something. What is changing is where they find it: no longer in bars or clubs, but in spaces where people gather around something shared — whether a passion, a trip, or a dinner with people they have not yet met.

Kindred

The most important relationships rarely begin online. They begin over a shared meal, in a spontaneous conversation, when a friend introduces you to someone at a bar, at the gym, or at an exhibition. When you are physically present with people, defences drop and trust builds naturally and faster. We are wired to welcome others and to feel part of something, but modern life has pulled us away from that instinct, optimising everything for efficiency and convenience. What people seek today is no longer content. It is someone to be with. At Kindred we see this every day through our members' stories: give people the right context and the community does not need to be built. It happens on its own.

Justine Palefsky — CEO & Co-founder, Kindred

GetYourGuide

Today, travelling means creating genuine relationships, not simply ticking places off a list. We see this especially when people do something together: kneading bread, learning a craft, sharing a table, swapping stories with a local guide. This is also why workshops are one of the fastest-growing categories on GetYourGuide: bookings have increased by 59% year on year. Experiences are not just about showing you a place. They give you a reason to talk, laugh, take part, and feel part of something, even if only for an afternoon.

Jean-Gabriel Duveau — VP Brand & Strategic Partnerships, GetYourGuide

But the relationships born while travelling do not end when you return home. For many people they last, precisely because they are rooted in something lived and shared — not a profile or a match. Those who have shared a place, a moment, a stretch of a journey with someone, often remain connected far longer than they expected.

This is the same idea that Fabio Bin, co-founder and CMO of WeRoad, wrote about in in March 2026, regarding what we do at WeRoad:

WeRoad

We always thought we were in the travel business, but in reality we respond to a much more human need. The point is not the destination — it is the people. When you bring together 15 strangers for ten days, away from their usual routine, barriers drop far more quickly than they do in everyday life.

Fabio Bin — Co-founder & CMO, WeRoad

Stories from the community.

We asked six members of our community to share their experience and how travel has had an impact on their lives and their relationships. Click on a card to read the full story.

Anna Harvey 🇬🇧

Anna is 31, British, works as a copywriter and describes herself as strongly introverted. She has never particularly enjoyed being around people. Despite this, she decided to challenge herself with a group trip to Nepal.

On a group trip, you go from the "performative" to the "human" within a matter of days. Labels fade away — and what remains are the people.

read more →
Anna Harvey 🇬🇧
"In general, I'm not a big fan of people. When my social battery runs out, I tend to disconnect quickly. But I discovered that the intimate context of a group trip accelerates everything. You go from the 'performative' to the 'human' within a matter of days. Labels disappear. At some point you realise that the gym guy has the same sense of humour as you and that the doctor in the group is as obsessed with hipster coffee as you are. In the end we are all twenty-, thirty-, forty-somethings trying to navigate this slightly absurd world. It was proof that even those who tend to avoid people can find their place in the right context. We are still in touch even though we live on the other side of the world. And the most wonderful thing is that when we meet again it feels as if no time has passed at all."
Alicia Bailac 🇪🇸

Alicia is 39, Spanish, works as a management assistant and is a trip coordinator. Curious by nature but held back by fears, after being ghosted before a trip she decided to go anyway.

He ghosted me before the trip. I booked it anyway, alone, to Jordan — and found people who pushed me beyond every limit I thought I had.

read more →
Alicia Bailac 🇪🇸
"I was supposed to travel with my ex. He ghosted me. I had no one to go with, but I decided that I wasn't going to stay home and cry. I booked a group trip to Jordan, a place I knew nothing about. I was afraid of heights, diving, canyoning — practically everything. But when you are surrounded by people who don't know you, who have no preconceived ideas about who you are or what you can't do, something changes. They waited for me, encouraged me, supported me. No judgement, just people pushing each other forward. And at some point I thought: yes, I can do this. That experience made me want to be the person who helps others to do the same, and that is why I became a coordinator. My best friends today come from this world — both coordinators and travellers. From my very first trip alone, two couples were formed and they are still together, more than three years on."
Manuel Ortega Sánchez 🇪🇸

Manuel is 35, from Seville, works as an aeronautical engineer and is a coordinator. He went on his first group trip after the pandemic and also found his partner, whom he is marrying next year.

While travelling you see a person in many different situations, naturally, without pressure. That is how I met Sheila — we are getting married next year.

read more →
Manuel Ortega Sánchez 🇪🇸
"After Covid, none of my friends wanted to travel. So I booked a trip to Mexico without knowing anyone. On the first day I thought 'what am I doing here?'. Two days later I thought 'this is the best thing I've done in years'. Then came Jordan. And Sheila. We started talking because during an icebreaker game she said she wanted to go to Australia, and for me that country had been the most beautiful experience of my life. When you travel you see another person in so many different situations, naturally, without pressure. You get to know them from the inside. That doesn't happen on an app, where you always start with the same questions and everything feels a little contrived. If I hadn't met her on that trip, we probably wouldn't be together. We're getting married next year."

The strongest connections were not forged at sunset in Bali. They were forged on a 15-hour bus ride across the Andes, when nobody was trying to impress anyone.

Simon Serp 🇫🇷

Daniela D'Attolico 🇮🇹

When I finally went to Norway, something changed. Being in a group where nobody knows you means no fear of being judged. You find the courage to do things you would not do on your own. If I could go back, I would do it sooner, because there are no real reasons to feel anxious or afraid. When I got back, I told my therapist about the trip with tears in my eyes. She was amazed. I was going through a difficult time and that trip gave me strength and the desire to keep hoping for a better future. I am still in touch with my fellow travellers. 07 Conclusion
What this research truly reveals.

People have not stopped seeking each other out.

If we were to summarise everything we have gathered over these months, we would probably start here: people have not stopped seeking each other out. They are simply changing the places, the ways, and the contexts in which it happens.

And these are the figures that, above all others, tell us what is happening.
  • 0% of people say that meeting someone new today is harder than it used to be. A figure that has grown by 8 points in one year.
  • Travel has become the number-one context in which people feel they get to know others best: 0%, compared with 30% in 2025.
  • Apps and social media remain last in the ranking of places where meaningful relationships are born: only 0% consider them effective.
  • 0% of respondents would like to have more offline social experiences.
  • 0% feel the need to belong to a group built around shared passions and interests.
  • 0% are not satisfied with their social relationships.
  • 0% say they feel more open towards others when travelling.
  • Two out of three people have built an important bond with someone they met while travelling.
  • For 0% what creates connection above all is shared experiences, not simply the time spent in the same place.

The desire to connect has never gone away. What is increasingly lacking are the right contexts for it to happen — and this is where travel, for many people, is beginning to make the difference.
